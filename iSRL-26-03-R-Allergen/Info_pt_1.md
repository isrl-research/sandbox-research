## 1. What This Is and Why It Matters

This document is your guide to contributing to a real research report that will be published under iSRL on Zenodo with a DOI. That means it will be permanently citable, publicly accessible, and carry your name if you meet the co-authorship criteria in Section 6.

The report is titled **Allergen Declaration in India — Rules, Reality, and Gaps** (ID: `iSRL-26-03-R-Allergen`). It is divided into five sub-parts, SP01 through SP05. You have been assigned one sub-part and will work on it with one other person as a duo — independently until specific points in the process, described in Section 3.

This is not a class assignment. The quality standard is that of a research output a lawyer in Delhi or a food company in Bangalore should be able to use. That said — you are not expected to already know how to do this. You are expected to be honest about what you know and what you don't, to look things up before asking, and to do the actual reading yourself rather than delegating it.

## 3. The Duo Structure

You are working in a pair. You will both work on the same sub-part, independently, until the milestone submission window opens. This is intentional. Two people working independently and then comparing findings catch more gaps than one person working twice as long.

**What independent means in practice:**
- You do not share notes, findings, or drafts with your partner until the milestone submission window opens
- You do not ask your partner which sources they found or what they think about a specific document
- You may discuss the topic in general terms — what a word means, whether a document exists — but not your interpretation of it

After each milestone, once both of you have submitted, you can read each other's reports. The point is to notice what your partner caught that you didn't — and carry that forward into the next milestone, not copy it.

The sealed submission for Milestone 4 works differently — see Section 5.

## 5. Milestone Plan

The sprint runs from **March 9 to March 20**. Six milestones. Every milestone ends with a report submitted to your private repository.

Budget approximately **4–5 hours per milestone**. In every block of work, keep the last hour for writing the report based on what you did. Do not leave the writing for after — writing as you go is how you catch gaps in your own understanding.

*Setup only. Nothing research-related yet.*

1. Create your GitHub account
2. Create your private repository (see Section 4)
3. Create an Overleaf account at [overleaf.com](https://www.overleaf.com). You do not need to know LaTeX yet — Part 2 has a minimal template that shows you what you need
4. Read Part 1 fully and skim Part 2
5. Submit the **Milestone 0 Google Form** (link shared separately). It will ask for your GitHub ID, your confidence ratings across relevant skills, and your **magic sentence** — you will know what that means once you have read this document carefully

### Milestone 1 — March 9 to 11

*Orient yourself. Do not look for sources yet.*

Read your sub-part description in the GitHub issue fully. Then, working independently:

1. List every word or concept in the sub-part description you do not fully understand. Look each one up. Write a short definition in your own words
2. Write out, in plain language, what questions your sub-part is trying to answer — not copied from the issue, your own restatement of it
3. Write down where you think the answers might live. What kind of document would have this? What organisation would have published it? You do not need to find the documents yet — just map the territory

**Your Milestone 1 report contains these three things.** Once both you and your partner have submitted, you will read each other's reports. Before moving to Milestone 2, note anything your partner mapped that you missed.

*This milestone is the most uncomfortable one.* Sitting with a question you can't immediately answer and mapping it before you start searching is not a natural instinct. It is a skill. You are doing well even if the report feels incomplete — it is supposed to feel that way at this stage.

---

### Milestone 2 — March 12 to 14

*Find your sources. Do not start reading them in depth yet.*

> *The magic letter from this section: find the only letter that appears in "apple" and "stone" but does not appear in "clown." That is your magic letter for this section.*

1. For each question your sub-part needs to answer, find the primary source document that contains the answer. Part 2 explains what a primary source is and how to find one
2. For each source you find, write one line: what specific question does this document answer, and exactly where in the document (page number, regulation number, section, clause)
3. Build a running definition list — every technical or legal term you had to look up goes here with its definition. There may be dozens. That is fine
4. Note where you hit dead ends — where you expected a source to exist but could not find it. Dead ends are part of your findings

**Your Milestone 2 report contains:** the source list, the definition list, and the dead ends. Same process — submit to private repo, wait for partner confirmation, read each other's before Milestone 3.

---

### Milestone 3 — March 15 to 17

*Read the sources. Extract what is relevant.*

1. Go through each primary source from Milestone 2. Pull the exact excerpts that answer your questions — copy the text, include the page or clause reference
2. For each excerpt, write one sentence explaining why it answers the question you had
3. Distinguish clearly between what the source states directly and what you are inferring from it. Both are valid — but they must be labelled differently
4. Note explicitly where the sources do not answer a question. *"No published data was found on X"* is a research finding
5. Secondary sources are for helping you understand a primary source — they are not citable. If you used one to understand something, note it but do not include it in your citation list

**Your Milestone 3 report** contains the extracted excerpts, your explanations, and an explicit account of what you could not find. From this milestone onward, include a **claims list** (in the appendix) and a **paper table** (as a separate file). Part 2 explains both.

Write your complete draft for the sub-part following the full submission format described in Part 2.

Submit to your private repository **without reading your partner's work**. Do not contact your partner about the draft. Once I confirm both submissions are in, I will ping you both to open PRs to the main repository. At that point, you may read each other's drafts.

### Milestone 5 — March 19

*Peer review two other duos' sub-parts.*

Once PRs are open, each duo reviews two other duos' sub-parts. This is verification, not editing. For each claim in the report, check whether the cited source actually supports it. Raise anything that does not hold up in the GitHub Discussion thread with the specific claim and source.

Always reference the point, not the person. Praise can be directed at the person. Critique is always directed at the specific line.

---

### Milestone 6 — March 20

*Incorporate review feedback. Final submission.*

Address peer review comments from Milestone 5. Update your report and paper table where needed. Submit the final version as a PR.

The two contributors who showed the strongest writing across all milestones will be asked to serve as lead editors for the final stitched report.

## 6. Co-Authorship Criteria

> *The magic letter from this section is the letter that sits exactly in the middle of the word "contribution" — count carefully.*

To be listed as a co-author on the final published report, a contributor must:

1. Complete their sub-part's standalone report (Milestones 1–4) and submit the final version (Milestone 6)
2. Peer review at least two other duos' sub-parts (Milestone 5)

Contributions that do not meet these criteria will be acknowledged in the acknowledgments section. Every genuine contribution is valued — co-authorship is not the only measure of it.
