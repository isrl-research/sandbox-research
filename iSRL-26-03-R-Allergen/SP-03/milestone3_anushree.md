*3. are "may contain" / precautionary labels required, regulated, or voluntary?*
* Source: [FASTER Act of 2021](https://www.fda.gov/food/nutrition-food-labeling-and-critical-foods/food-allergies), [Link1](https://www.fda.gov/media/117410/download)
* Excerpt:
  * `D.15 Is it sufficient to just label my food product with a “may contains” statement due to possible cross-contact? No. You must follow current good manufacturing practices to protect against allergen cross- contact (see 21 CFR part 117, specifically sections 117.10(b), 117.20(b)(2), 117.35 (d)-(f), 117.40(a)-(b), 117.80(a)-(c), 117.93)). Food facilities that are subject to the preventive controls requirements in 21 CFR part 117 must conduct a hazard analysis and implement a food safety plan that may include allergen controls for proper labeling and the protection of food from allergen cross-contact (see 21 CFR 117.135(c)(2) in the Current Good Manufacturing Practice, Hazard Analysis, and Risk Based Preventive Controls for Human Food). While allergen advisory statements that are truthful and not misleading may be acceptable in certain circumstances, they cannot be used in lieu of good manufacturing practices and preventive controls.`
* Why this answers the question: This excerpt directly explains the regulatory status of “may contain” (precautionary) labeling by clarifying whether it is sufficient, required, or optional in managing allergen risks.
* What the source directly states:
  * “May contain” (advisory) statements are not sufficient on their own to address allergen risk.
  * Food manufacturers must follow good manufacturing practices and preventive controls instead.
  * Advisory labeling may be used only if it is truthful and not misleading.
* What I infer: Precautionary allergen labeling is voluntary and not legally required, but it is indirectly regulated because it must not mislead consumers and cannot replace proper safety controls.

* What the source directly states:

    * The Secretary determines it is sufficient to protect public health
    * The approval is formally published
* What I infer: 
  * The default requirement is on-package labeling, ensuring visibility to consumers
  * Any deviation from this standard is strictly controlled and exceptional, reinforcing the importance of clear and accessible allergen information
