## **Part A: European Union**

**Source List**

The following sections document the specific provisions of Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 that were examined in order to understand how allergen information must be communicated to consumers in the European Union.

**Source 1**

**Document:** Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 (consolidated version available on EUR-Lex)

**Question answered:** Which foods or substances must legally be declared as allergens on food labels?

**Location in document:** Annex II – "Substances or products causing allergies or intolerances"

**Notes:** Annex II provides the official list of allergens that require mandatory declaration under EU food labelling law. The regulation identifies 14 allergen categories. These include cereals containing gluten, specifically wheat, rye, barley, oats and their hybridised strains. They include crustaceans, eggs, fish, peanuts, soybeans, and milk. The list also includes nuts such as almonds, hazelnuts, walnuts, cashews, pecans, Brazil nuts, pistachios, and macadamia nuts. Celery, mustard, sesame seeds, lupin, and molluscs are also listed. Sulphur dioxide and sulphites must be declared when present at concentrations exceeding 10 mg/kg or 10 mg/l.

The annex also lists certain exceptions. For example, fully refined soybean oil and wheat-based glucose syrups are excluded from mandatory declaration. These products originate from allergenic sources, but the processing they undergo removes the proteins responsible for allergic reactions. This shows that the regulation does not simply list ingredients but also takes into account scientific evidence about allergenic risk.

**Source 2**

**Document:** Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011

**Question answered:** Whether allergens must be declared when present in small quantities.

**Location in document:** Article 9(1)(c) – List of mandatory particulars

**Notes:** Article 9 establishes the core set of information that must appear on food labels. Point (c) states that labels must indicate any ingredient or processing aid listed in Annex II that is used in the manufacture or preparation of a food and still present in the finished product. The provision explicitly requires declaration even when the allergen remains in an altered form.

Importantly, the article does not specify any minimum quantity threshold. The requirement therefore appears to depend on the presence of the allergen, rather than the amount contained in the food product. This indicates that EU food labelling law adopts a presence-based approach to allergen disclosure.

**Source 3**

**Document:** Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011

**Question answered:** How allergens must appear in the ingredients list.

**Location in document:** Article 21 – Labelling of certain substances or products causing allergies or intolerances

**Notes:**

Article 21 establishes how allergens must be displayed so that consumers can easily identify them. The regulation requires that allergen names be visually distinguished from the rest of the ingredients list. Possible methods of distinction include bold type, italic text, different font style, or contrasting background colour.

Article 21 operates together with Article 18, which governs the general structure of ingredient lists. This means allergen highlighting occurs within the ingredient list that is already ordered by weight. For foods that do not contain an ingredients list, the regulation requires the label to include the word "contains" followed by the allergen name. If the food name itself clearly indicates the allergen, such as peanut butter, a separate allergen declaration may not be required.

**Source 4**

**Document:** Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011

**Question answered:** What rules apply to label format and readability.

**Location in document:** Article 13 – Presentation of mandatory particulars

**Notes:** Article 13 regulates how mandatory information must physically appear on packaging. According to the article, required information must be placed in a conspicuous location, be easily visible, be clearly legible, and not be hidden or interrupted by other text or images.

The regulation defines minimum font sizes using the concept of x-height, which refers to the height of the lowercase letter "x" in a given typeface. The minimum requirements are 1.2 mm x-height for standard packaging and 0.9 mm x-height for packages with a surface area smaller than 80 cm². Using x-height instead of point size ensures consistent readability across different fonts.

Article 13 also requires certain key information, including the name of the food, net quantity, and alcohol strength where applicable, to appear within the same field of vision.

**Source 5**

**Document:** Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011

**Question answered:** How ingredients must generally be listed.

**Location in document:** Article 18 – List of ingredients

**Notes:** Article 18 establishes the general rules governing ingredient lists. All ingredients must be listed in descending order of weight, measured at the time they are used in the manufacturing process. This ordering allows consumers to understand the relative proportion of ingredients in the product. Article 21 relies on the structure established in Article 18, meaning allergen emphasis takes place within this weight-based ingredient list.

**Source 6**

**Document:** Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011

**Question answered:** Whether allergen declaration requirements apply to food sold without packaging.

**Location in document:** Article 44 – National measures for non-prepacked food

**Notes:** Article 44 addresses foods that are not prepacked, such as items sold loose in bakeries, markets, or deli counters. The article confirms that the allergen information required under Article 9(1)(c) remains mandatory for these foods. However, the regulation allows Member States to determine how the information must be communicated. Possible approaches include written notices or signs, information booklets, or verbal communication provided by staff. This flexibility allows national authorities to adapt allergen communication rules to different retail environments.

### **Definition List (EU)**

The regulation uses several technical terms that are important for understanding its provisions.

**Ingredient** means any substance used in the manufacture or preparation of a food that remains present in the finished product, even if its form has changed during processing. This definition is found in Article 2(2)(f).

**Processing Aid** refers to a substance used during food processing for technological purposes that may leave residues in the final product but is not considered an ingredient. Processing aids derived from allergenic sources may still require declaration if residues remain.

**Food Business Operator** is the natural or legal person responsible for ensuring that food information complies with EU law for products under their control. This is defined in Article 8(1).

**Mandatory Food Information** means all information that must legally appear on food labels under EU legislation, as described in Article 2(2)(c).

**Annex** refers to a section appended to a regulation that provides detailed lists or technical specifications. Annexes have the same legal force as the main articles.

**Delegated Act** is a legal instrument allowing the European Commission to update non-essential elements of the regulation without the full legislative procedure, as outlined in Article 51\.

**Emphasised Text** means the visual distinction used to highlight allergens within the ingredients list. This can include bold text, italic formatting, or other typographic methods as required by Article 21\.

**X-height** is the height of lowercase letters such as "x" used to measure minimum font size in EU food labelling law. It is defined in Annex IV.

**List of Ingredients** is a section of the label that presents all ingredients used in a product in descending order of weight at the time of manufacture, as governed by Article 18\.

**Allergen** means a substance capable of causing allergic reactions or intolerances and listed in Annex II. Such substances must be declared when present in food products under Article 9(1)(c).

**Consolidated Version** refers to a version of legislation that incorporates the original regulation together with all subsequent amendments into a single document.

**Non-prepacked Food** means food sold without packaging, including loose items, food packed at the consumer's request, or products prepacked for direct sale on the same premises. This is addressed in Article 44\.

### **Dead Ends (EU)**

**Explicit Quantity Threshold**

The regulation was examined for provisions establishing a numerical threshold for allergen declaration, such as a minimum level expressed in milligrams per kilogram. No such threshold was identified. Article 9(1)(c) simply requires declaration of allergens that remain present in the finished product. Because the regulation does not qualify this requirement with any quantity limitation, allergen disclosure appears to follow a presence-based rule. The absence of a numerical threshold therefore appears to be intentional rather than accidental.

**Precautionary Allergen Labelling**

The regulation was also reviewed for provisions governing precautionary allergen statements such as "may contain nuts" or "may contain traces of peanuts". No article within Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 directly regulates these statements. Precautionary allergen labelling therefore appears to operate on a voluntary basis, with guidance potentially provided through other EU documents rather than binding provisions in this regulation.

**Summary of Findings (EU)**

Analysis of Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 indicates several key conclusions regarding allergen labelling in the European Union.

The mandatory allergen list appears in Annex II, which contains fourteen categories of allergens. Allergen declaration is presence-based, as no minimum quantity threshold is specified. Allergens must be visually emphasised within ingredient lists under Article 21\. Minimum font sizes are defined using x-height measurements to ensure label readability. Non-prepacked foods must still provide allergen information under Article 44\. Precautionary allergen statements such as "may contain" are not regulated within this regulation.

Overall, the regulation aims to ensure that consumers, particularly those with food allergies or intolerances, receive clear, visible, and accessible information about the presence of allergenic substances in food products.

## **Part B: Australia/New Zealand**

### **Source List**

The following sections document the specific provisions of the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code that were examined in order to understand how allergen information must be communicated to consumers in Australia and New Zealand.

**Source 1**

**Document:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code – Standard 1.2.3 – Information requirements – warning statements, advisory statements and declarations

**Question answered:** Which foods or substances must legally be declared as allergens on food labels in Australia and New Zealand?

**Location in document:** Section 1.2.3—4 (Mandatory declarations of certain foods) and Schedule 9, section S9—3 (Mandatory declarations)

**Notes:** Section 1.2.3—4 establishes the requirement for mandatory declarations. It states that if a food listed in Column 1 of the table in section S9—3 is present in a food for sale, a declaration that the food is present is required. This applies whether the food is present as an ingredient, as part of a compound ingredient, as a food additive, or as a processing aid.

The table in section S9—3 lists the foods requiring mandatory declaration. These include added sulphites at concentrations of 10 mg/kg or more. They include cereals containing gluten, namely barley, oats, rye, and wheat including their hybridised strains. The table also lists tree nuts: almond, Brazil nut, cashew, hazelnut, macadamia, pecan, pine nut, pistachio, and walnut. Crustacea, egg, fish, lupin, milk, mollusc, peanut, sesame seed, and soybean are also included.

The table specifies exemptions in Column 2\. For fish, isinglass derived from fish swim bladders and used as a clarifying agent in beer or wine is exempt. For milk, alcohol distilled from whey is exempt. For soybean, highly refined soybean oil that has been degummed, neutralised, bleached and deodorised is exempt, as are soybean derivatives that are tocopherol or phytosterol. For wheat, glucose syrups made from wheat starch that have been refined to contain no more than 20 mg/kg of gluten are exempt, as is alcohol distilled from wheat.

**Source 2**

**Document:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code – Standard 1.2.3 – Information requirements – warning statements, advisory statements and declarations

**Question answered:** Is there a quantity threshold for allergen declaration, or is it quantity-independent?

**Location in document:** Section 1.2.3—4 (Mandatory declarations of certain foods) and Schedule 9, section S9—3

**Notes:** Section 1.2.3—4 requires declaration if a food listed in the table is present in a food for sale. There is no mention of any minimum quantity threshold in the main provisions of Standard 1.2.3. The requirement appears to apply regardless of the amount present, as long as the food is present as an ingredient, component of a compound ingredient, food additive, or processing aid.

However, two exceptions were noted where quantity does matter. Added sulphites are required to be declared only at concentrations of 10 mg/kg or more, as specified in Schedule 9, section S9—3, Item 1\. Below this concentration, declaration is not required. Additionally, glucose syrups made from wheat starch are exempt from declaration only if they have been refined to contain gluten protein content that does not exceed 20 mg/kg, as stated in Schedule 9, section S9—3, Item 3, Column 2\.

Apart from these specific exceptions, no general quantity threshold applies. For most allergens, if the substance is present at all, it must be declared.

**Source 3**

**Document:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code – Standard 1.2.3 – Information requirements – warning statements, advisory statements and declarations

**Question answered:** How are precautionary allergen labelling statements regulated?

**Location in document:** Section 1.2.3—2 (Mandatory advisory statements), Section 1.2.3—3 (Mandatory warning statement—royal jelly), and Schedule 9, section S9—2

**Notes:**

Section 1.2.3—2 deals with mandatory advisory statements. Unlike the EU regulation where "may contain" statements are completely voluntary and unregulated, the FSANZ Code takes a different approach. The advisory statements listed in Schedule 9, section S9—2 are mandatory when certain conditions are met.

For allergens specifically, several mandatory advisory statements are relevant. Bee pollen and foods containing bee pollen as an ingredient require an advisory statement indicating that the product contains bee pollen which can cause severe allergic reactions. Similarly, propolis and foods containing propolis as an ingredient require an advisory statement indicating that the product contains propolis which can cause severe allergic reactions.

Royal jelly is covered separately in section 1.2.3—3, which requires a mandatory warning statement: "This product contains royal jelly which has been reported to cause severe allergic reactions and in rare cases, fatalities, especially in asthma and allergy sufferers."

For other precautionary statements like "may contain traces of nuts" that address cross-contamination, these are not explicitly mandated in Standard 1.2.3. Such statements appear to be voluntary, though their use may be guided by other provisions or industry practice.

**Source 4**

**Document:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code – Standard 1.2.3 – Information requirements – warning statements, advisory statements and declarations

**Question answered:** How must allergens be presented in the ingredients list? What formatting is required?

**Location in document:** Section 1.2.3—7 (Form of a mandatory declaration)

**Notes:**

Section 1.2.3—7 sets out specific formatting requirements for allergen declarations in the statement of ingredients. The required name of the allergen must be printed in a bold font that provides a distinct contrast with any other text in the statement of ingredients which is not a required name. The font size must be not less than the size of type of the other text in the statement of ingredients.

Each allergen must be listed separately for each ingredient that contains it. For example, if a product contains both wheat flour and wheat starch, both must show the word "wheat" next to them. If the allergen name is contained within the name of the ingredient, such as "milk" in "buttermilk", it must be listed separately next to the ingredient name.

If there is no statement of ingredients, which is rare for packaged foods, a summary statement must be used. Section 1.2.3—7 requires that a summary statement commence with the word "Contains" followed by the required name of each allergen. It must contain no other words. The summary statement must appear in the same field of view as the statement of ingredients, directly next to it, and be distinctly separated from it. The summary statement must be printed in the same typeface and size of type as any required name in the statement of ingredients, and in a bold font that contrasts distinctly with other text.

**Source 5**

**Document:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code – Standard 1.2.1 – Requirements to have labels or otherwise provide information

**Question answered:** What are the general format requirements for labels, such as legibility and font size?

**Location in document:** Division 6 – Legibility requirements (sections 1.2.1—24 and 1.2.1—25)

**Notes:** Division 6 of Standard 1.2.1 sets out general legibility requirements that apply to all labels, including allergen information. Section 1.2.1—24 states that any word, statement, expression or design on a label must be in English. It must be legible. It must be prominent so as to contrast distinctly with the background of the label. If a language other than English is also used, the information in that language must not negate or contradict the English information.

Section 1.2.1—25 specifies minimum font sizes specifically for warning statements, such as the royal jelly warning mentioned in Source 3\. For a small package, the font size must be at least 1.5 mm. In all other cases, the font size must be at least 3 mm.

It is worth noting that these font size requirements apply specifically to warning statements, not to all allergen declarations. Allergen declarations in the ingredients list must follow the formatting rules in Standard 1.2.3—7, which require bold font and size not less than surrounding text, but do not specify an absolute minimum size in millimetres.

**Source 6**

**Document:** Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code – Standard 1.2.4 – Information requirements – statement of ingredients

**Question answered:** How does the general ingredients listing framework support allergen declaration requirements?

**Location in document:** Various sections, particularly section 1.2.4—4 and the note referencing Division 3 of Standard 1.2.3

**Notes:** Standard 1.2.4 provides the general rules for statements of ingredients, which interact with the specific allergen declaration rules in Standard 1.2.3. Section 1.2.4—4 includes an important note stating that, subject to Division 3 of Standard 1.2.3, a statement of ingredients must identify each ingredient. This confirms that the allergen declaration requirements in Standard 1.2.3 take precedence over the general ingredient naming rules.

Section 1.2.4—5(6)(b)(i) requires that even when a compound ingredient makes up less than five percent of a food, any ingredient of that compound ingredient that is required to be declared under section 1.2.3—4 must still be listed. This ensures that allergens are not hidden within small amounts of compound ingredients.

Section 1.2.4—3(2)(d) and (e) exempt processing aids from being listed in the statement of ingredients generally. However, section 1.2.3—4(5)(c) specifically requires that processing aids derived from allergenic sources must still be declared, overriding this general exemption.

### **Definition List (FSANZ)**

Examination of the FSANZ standards revealed several terms requiring clarification.

**Required name** refers to the specific name that must be used when declaring an allergen, as specified in Columns 3 and 4 of the table in section S9—3. This definition appears in Standard 1.2.3—1A and Schedule 9 S9—3.

**Size of type** means the measurement from the base to the top of a letter or numeral. It is used in legibility requirements throughout the standards and is defined in Standard 1.2.3—1A.

**Warning statement** is a mandatory statement alerting consumers to a serious risk associated with a food. Warning statements have specific font size requirements in Standard 1.2.1—25. The royal jelly warning in section 1.2.3—3 is an example.

**Advisory statement** is a mandatory statement providing information about a food, such as the presence of certain substances that may cause allergic reactions in some individuals. Advisory statements are covered in Standard 1.2.3—2 and listed in Schedule 9 S9—2.

**Summary statement** is a statement beginning with the word "Contains" that lists allergens when there is no statement of ingredients. It must appear next to the statement of ingredients in the same field of view, as required by Standard 1.2.3—7.

**Compound ingredient** is an ingredient that is itself made up of multiple ingredients. Allergens from compound ingredients must still be declared, as outlined in Standard 1.2.4—5.

**Processing aid** is a substance used during processing for a technological purpose that may leave residues in the final product. Processing aids from allergenic sources require declaration under section 1.2.3—4, even though they are generally exempt from ingredient listing under Standard 1.3.3.

**Small package** refers to a package with a surface area of less than 100 cm². Such packages have specific labelling exemptions under Standard 1.2.4—2.

**Field of view** means the area of a package that can be seen from a single viewing point. The summary statement must be in the same field of view as the statement of ingredients, as required by Standard 1.2.3—7.

**Gluten** is a protein composite found in certain cereals, including wheat, rye, barley, and oats. It must be declared when present, though exemptions exist for highly refined derivatives such as glucose syrups with gluten content not exceeding 20 mg/kg, as specified in Schedule 9, section S9—3.

### **Dead Ends (FSANZ)**

**Explicit Quantity Threshold for Most Allergens**

When examining Standard 1.2.3 and Schedule 9, an explicit quantity threshold for allergen declaration was expected but not found for most allergens. Section 1.2.3—4 requires declaration if an allergen is present in the food, without qualification. This suggests that for the majority of allergens, including cereals, tree nuts, crustacea, egg, fish, lupin, milk, mollusc, peanut, sesame, and soybean, declaration is required regardless of the amount present, as long as the substance is used as an ingredient, component of a compound ingredient, food additive, or processing aid.

The only exceptions identified were added sulphites, which require declaration only at concentrations of 10 mg/kg or more, and glucose syrups from wheat starch, which are exempt only if gluten content does not exceed 20 mg/kg. This partial finding is recorded as a dead end in the sense that a general threshold applicable to all allergens was expected but not found.

**Precautionary Labelling for Cross-Contamination**

A search was conducted for provisions specifically regulating "may contain" statements that address cross-contamination risk, such as "may contain traces of peanuts" or "produced in a facility that also processes nuts". No such provisions were identified in Standard 1.2.3, Schedule 9, or Standard 1.2.1.

Standard 1.2.3—2 and Schedule 9—2 do mandate certain advisory statements, but these are specific to particular ingredients such as bee pollen and propolis, and are not general "may contain" provisions. The royal jelly warning in section 1.2.3—3 is also specific rather than general.

This suggests that precautionary allergen labelling for cross-contamination risk is not explicitly regulated in these standards and may be voluntary, though its use might be guided by other instruments or industry practice. This is recorded as a dead end.

**Absolute Minimum Font Size for Allergen Declarations**

Standard 1.2.3—7 requires allergen declarations to be in a bold font with size not less than the size of type of the other text. This establishes a relative requirement, meaning allergens must be at least as large as surrounding text, but it does not specify an absolute minimum in millimetres.

Standard 1.2.1—25 does specify absolute minimum font sizes of 1.5 mm for small packages and 3 mm otherwise, but this applies specifically to warning statements such as the royal jelly warning, not to allergen declarations generally. Whether allergen declarations in the ingredients list are subject to these absolute minimums is not entirely clear from the text.

A definitive answer on whether there is an absolute minimum font size in millimetres for routine allergen declarations in the ingredients list remains unresolved. This is recorded as a partial dead end.

### **Summary of Findings (FSANZ)**

Examination of the FSANZ Food Standards Code, including Standard 1.2.3, Standard 1.2.1, Standard 1.2.4, and Schedule 9, yielded the following conclusions.

The mandatory allergen list appears in Schedule 9, section S9—3. It comprises cereals containing gluten, tree nuts, crustacea, egg, fish, lupin, milk, mollusc, peanut, sesame, soybean, and added sulphites. Specific exemptions are provided for highly refined derivatives such as fully refined soybean oil and glucose syrups from wheat starch with very low gluten content.

Allergen declaration is quantity-independent for most substances, requiring declaration if the allergen is present as an ingredient, compound ingredient component, food additive, or processing aid. The only exceptions are added sulphites with a threshold of 10 mg/kg or more, and glucose syrups from wheat starch which are exempt if gluten content does not exceed 20 mg/kg.

Precautionary allergen labelling is addressed through mandatory advisory statements for specific substances including bee pollen and propolis, and a mandatory warning statement for royal jelly. General "may contain" statements for cross-contamination risk are not explicitly regulated in these standards.

Allergens must be declared in the statement of ingredients using a bold font that contrasts distinctly with other text, with font size not less than the surrounding text. Where no statement of ingredients exists, a summary statement beginning with the word "Contains" must appear next to the statement of ingredients in the same field of view.

General legibility requirements in Standard 1.2.1 mandate that all label information be in English, legible, and prominent with distinct contrast from the background. Warning statements have specific minimum font sizes of 1.5 mm for small packages and 3 mm otherwise, though this does not clearly apply to all allergen declarations.

Standard 1.2.4 reinforces that allergen declaration requirements take precedence over general ingredient listing rules, ensuring allergens are declared even when present in small amounts or as processing aids.

## **Overall Summary**

Comparing the two jurisdictions, several similarities and differences emerge. Both the EU and FSANZ require mandatory declaration of a core set of allergens, though the FSANZ list includes some additional items like lupin and distinguishes added sulphites with a specific threshold. Both require allergens to be visually emphasised in the ingredients list, though the formatting rules differ slightly in detail. The EU uses x-height to define minimum font sizes for all mandatory information, while FSANZ specifies absolute font sizes only for warning statements and relies on relative sizing for routine allergen declarations. Both jurisdictions require allergen information for non-prepacked foods, though the EU allows Member States flexibility in implementation while FSANZ provides more specific rules through the Code.
