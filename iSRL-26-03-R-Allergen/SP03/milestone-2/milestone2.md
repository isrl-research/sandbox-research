## Sources list

1. UNITED STATES 
	i. which allergens must be declared on food labels?
		- primary source 1: [Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA)](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-allergensgluten-free-guidance-documents-regulatory-information/food-allergen-labeling-and-consumer-protection-act-2004-falcpa)
			- what it answers: the list of "major food allergens" that must be declared on labels (initially 8: milk, egg, fish, Crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, wheat, peanuts, soybeans), under SEC. 202. FINDINGS.
		- primary source 2: [FASTER Act of 2021 (Food Allergy Safety, Treatment, Education, and Research Act)](https://www.fda.gov/food/nutrition-food-labeling-and-critical-foods/food-allergies)
			- what it answers: that sesame must be declared as a major allergen from the effective date (Jan 1, 2023) onward, under "What’s New"
	ii. are allergens labeled regardless of quantity or only above thresholds?
		- primary source: same FALCPA statutory text and FDA Q&A/guidance on "major food allergens"
			- what it answers: does FALCPA require declaration whenever a major allergen is present, or only above a threshold
	iii. are "may contain" / precautionary labels required, regulated, or voluntary?
		- primary source: FDA guidance documents on advisory statements like "may contain" and "processed in a facility that also processes…" (often under "Food Allergies" and labeling guidance for industry)
			- what it answers: clarify that precautionary statements are not required by FALCPA but are subject to general misbranding rules
	iv. how must allergen information appear on the package?
		- primary source: FALCPA text again, because it spells out how the word “Contains” must be used or how allergens appear in the ingredient list
		- FDA Q&A and guidance restate and clarify formatting (placement near ingredient list, plain English names, etc.)

2. JAPAN 
- NOTE: PAL IS PROHIBITED IN JAPAN (allegedly, not sure)
- NOTE: USDA FAS report is not a truly primary source
	i. which allergens must be declared on food labels?
		- primary source 1: [Food Labeling Act plus] (https://www.japaneselawtranslation.go.jp/en/laws/view/2601/en)
			- what it answers: that allergen information is a required labeling item, and that there is a list of specified ingredients to be disclosed, chaper 2,3,4
	ii. are allergens labeled regardless of quantity or only above thresholds?
		- primary source: [USDA/FAS report "Japan – An Overview of the Food Labeling Standard" (2014)](https://www.fas.usda.gov/data/japan-overview-food-labeling-standard)
			- what it answers: it lists the seven as egg, milk, buckwheat, wheat, peanuts, crab, shrimp/prawn, and the 20 "recommended" items, notes that the "Standard requires that all allergens be individually labeled (e.g., dried egg yolk (including egg))"
	iii. are "may contain" / precautionary labels required, regulated, or voluntary?
		- primary source: Consumer Affairs Agency guidance and standards relating to voluntary allergen labeling
			- what it answers:  USDA/FAS overview hints that previous law allowed omission of potential allergens and that new standards require individual labeling, which is about mandatory ingredients
	iv. how must allergen information appear on the package?
		- primary source: Food Labeling Act and implementing standards govern what must be displayed and how (placement, font, etc.), USDA/FAS overview references requirements like “all allergens be individually labeled” and may describe placement rules
