# SP05: India-Specific Food Allergen Analysis
## Introduction
Food allergen regulation in India is currently guided by the FSSAI list of eight major allergens: cereals containing gluten,
 crustaceans, eggs, fish, peanuts, soybeans, milk, and tree nuts. While these align with global regulatory standards,
there is increasing evidence that India’s dietary diversity and regional food patterns necessitate a more context-specific
understanding of allergen prevalence.

## Availability of India-Specific Evidence
Contrary to earlier assumptions, India-specific allergen studies do exist (Mahesh et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020; Wong et al., 2010). 
However, the available evidence is geographically limited and methodologically heterogeneous, preventing the construction of a 
definitive national allergen profile.
Key sources include:
+ EuroPrevall-INCO studies (adults and children) (Mahesh et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020)
+ Regional clinical and survey-based studies (e.g., Delhi NCR) (Sehgal et al., 2024)
+ Indian Academy of Pediatrics (IAP) Delphi Consensus (2026) (Nagarajan et al., 2026)

These studies provide valuable insights but are concentrated in specific regions such as Karnataka and Delhi NCR, with 
limited nationwide representation.

## Limitations in Current Evidence
The current body of literature exhibits several constraints:
+ Geographic concentration (e.g., South India, urban centers)
+ Variation in methodology (self-reported surveys vs clinical diagnosis)
+ Differences in diagnostic criteria (sensitisation vs confirmed allergy)
+ Limited large-scale, nationally representative datasets
As a result, while evidence exists, it is insufficient for forming a unified national allergen prioritization framework.

## Emerging India-Specific Allergen Candidates
Beyond the FSSAI-mandated allergens, several food items consistently appear across independent Indian studies.
### High-Priority Candidates
+ Sesame
+ Brinjal (eggplant)
+ Legumes (lentil, chickpea, black gram)
+ Shellfish (prawn)
+ Banana
These allergens reflect Indian dietary patterns and regional consumption habits.

## Sensitisation vs Clinical Allergy Gap
A notable trend across Indian studies is the significant gap between sensitisation and clinically confirmed allergy 
(Li et al., 2020; Mahesh et al., 2016):
+ Children: ~19.1% sensitisation vs ~0.14% clinical allergy
+ Adults: ~26.5% sensitisation vs ~1.2% clinical allergy

### Possible Explanations
+ High dietary exposure leading to oral tolerance
+ Cross-reactivity (e.g., pollen-food syndrome)
+ Effects of traditional cooking methods reducing allergenicity
+ Microbiome diversity influencing immune response
+ Overestimation through skin prick or IgE-based testing
This gap highlights the importance of distinguishing between immunological sensitisation and clinically relevant allergic 
reactions in policy decisions.

## Global Context
Global studies provide a useful benchmark for comparison (Boye, 2012). While international allergen lists are dominated 
by milk, eggs, peanuts, and tree nuts, Indian data suggests additional region-specific allergens such as sesame and brinjal 
may warrant regulatory attention. Global studies provide a useful benchmark for comparison. While international allergen 
lists are dominated by milk, eggs, peanuts, and tree nuts, Indian data suggests additional region-specific allergens such

## Conclusion
India-specific allergen research, though limited, provides clear evidence that the current regulatory framework may not fully
capture regionally relevant allergens.
Future work should focus on large-scale, nationally representative studies and standardized diagnostic methodologies to 
better inform allergen regulation in India.
