# Milestone 3 Report  
## SP01 - Allergen Declaration Rules 
**Disha Rajpurohit**
## Overview:

- Purpose of Analysis: Reviewed FSSAI regulations and Delhi High Court ruling to understand allergen labeling rules in India.

- 2020 Labelling Regulations: Reg 5(14) lists eight allergens that must be declared: gluten-cereals, crustaceans, milk, eggs, fish, peanuts/tree nuts, soy, and sulphites ≥10 mg/kg.

- Labeling Requirements: Labels must say “Contains [Name]” for any of these allergens. Sulphites below 10 mg/kg do not need labeling.

- Optional Cross-Contamination: Manufacturers can optionally use “May contain [Allergen]” if cross-contamination is possible.

- Display and Language Rules: All allergen declarations must be on the principal display panel in English or Hindi (Devanagari) with minimum font sizes.

- Comparison:  
  - 2011 regulations allowed sulphites up to 1.5 g/kg without mandatory labeling.  
  - 2020 regulations require “Contains Sulphite” at ≥10 mg.  

- Court Ruling: The Delhi High Court said any ingredient from animals must be listed on the label, even in very small amounts, because it makes the product non-vegetarian.

## 1. Mandatory Allergen Declarations

#### Q: Which substances are listed as mandatory allergen declarations under current FSSAI rules?

Source:  [FSSAI Labelling and Display Regulations, 2020](https://fssai.gov.in/upload/uploadfiles/files/Comp_Labelling%20Display_Version%20VIII_09_09_2025.pdf), Regulation 5(14), page 18 [ point: (14) ]   
Claim :- ALLER.FSSAI.C.01  

### Direct excerpt:  

>  -  (14) Declaration regarding Food allergen:
>     
>      The following foods and ingredients which are known to cause allergy shall be declared separately as Contains............................ (Name of allergy causing ingredients)  
>
>      (i) Cereals containing gluten; i.e., wheat, rye, barley, oats, spelt or their hybridized strains and products of these (To be declared as name of the  cereal);  
>
>       2[except  
        (a) wheat based glucose syrups including dextrose*;  
        (b) wheat based maltodextrins*;  
        (c) glucose syrups based on barley;  
        (d) cereals used for making alcoholic distillates including ethyl alcohol of agricultural origin;  
>
>       Note: * The products thereof, provided these ingredients have been assessed as safe and gluten shall not be more than 20 mg/kg.]  
>
>     (ii) Crustacean and their products (To be declared as Crustacean);
>  
>      (iii) Milk & Milk products (To be declared as Milk);
>    
>      (iv) Eggs and egg products (To be declared as Egg);
>    
>      (v) Fish and fish products (To be declared as Fish);
>    
>      (vi) Peanuts, tree nuts (e.g. almonds, walnuts, pistachio, cashew nuts) and their products (To be declared as Nut);
>    
>      (vii) Soybeans and their products (To be declared as Soy);
>    
>      (viii) Sulphite in concentrations of 10mg/kg or more (To be declared as sulphite)                  

- #### Any of these ingredients must be listed on packaged foods. The only exception is certain gluten derivatives below 20 mg/kg.

## 2. Triggers: Thresholds and “May Contain”

#### Q: What triggers a declaration – is there a quantity threshold, a "may contain" provision, or is declaration required regardless of quantity?

Source: [FSSAI Labelling and Display Regulations, 2020](https://fssai.gov.in/upload/uploadfiles/files/Comp_Labelling%20Display_Version%20VIII_09_09_2025.pdf), Regulation 5(14), page 19 [ first paragraph ]     
Claim :- ALLER.FSSAI.C.02  

### Direct excerpt:  

>   - Provided that in case presence of ingredients due to cross contamination which are known to cause allergy may be declared separately as May Contains.......................... (Name of allergy causing ingredients).  
>
>     2[Provided further that this declaration is not required in case of oils and distilled alcoholic beverages derived from these ingredients and where the product itself is a food allergen.]  
>
>     Raw agricultural commodities are exempted from the allergen labelling requirements.  

- #### The only specified threshold is for sulphites: the rule states(direct excerpt) “(viii) Sulphite in concentrations of 10mg/kg or more (To be declared as sulphite)” must be declared.  

- #### For general allergens (i–vii), any detectable presence triggers “Contains” labeling. Sulphites must be declared if ≥10 mg/kg otherwise not.  

- #### Oils, distilled alcoholic beverages from listed allergens, and raw agricultural commodities are exempt from allergen labelling under Regulation 5(14) of the FSSAI Labelling and Display Regulations, 2020. However, the regulation does not give any specific quantity limit or processing rule for these exemptions. Unlike sulphites must be declared at 10 mg/kg or more, no such limit is given for oils or alcoholic beverages.

- #### For oils and distilled alcoholic beverages, the exemption is given based on the type of product, not on any specific quantity or processing level. The regulation does not clearly explain the reason for this exemption — whether it is because allergen proteins are removed during processing or simply because the law classifies these products as exempt. 

- #### For cross-contamination, manufacturers may declare “May contain [allergen]”. “Contains” requirements are direct; “may contain” is optional. Aside from sulphites, any presence of listed allergens requires labeling.

## 3. Label Format: Panel, Font, Language

#### Q: What is the prescribed format: where on the label, in what font size, in what language?

Sources: [FSSAI Labelling and Display Regulations, 2020](https://fssai.gov.in/upload/uploadfiles/files/Comp_Labelling%20Display_Version%20VIII_09_09_2025.pdf), Clause 4(5), page 4 & Table I, page 20   
Claim :- ALLER.FSSAI.C.03 & ALLER.FSSAI.C.04  

### Direct excerpt-1:  

>  - (5) The particulars of declaration required under these Regulations to be specified on the label shall be in English or Hindi in Devnagri script:  
>
>       Provided that nothing herein contained shall prevent the use of any other language in addition to the language required under this regulation:  
>
>       Provided further that the information provided in such other language shall not contradict the information on the label in the English or Hindi;  

### Direct excerpt-2:  

>  - (3) The height of any numeral and letter required under these regulations, on the principal display panel shall be as shown in table below:  
>
>       Table I: Minimum Height of Numerals and Letters
>
>      | Area of Principal Display Panel | Normal Case (mm) | When Blown, Moulded, or Perforated on Container (mm) |
>      |--------------------------------|------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
>      | Upto 200 cm²                   | 1                | 2                                                    |
>      | Above 200 cm² upto 500 cm²     | 2                | 4                                                    |
>      | Above 500 cm² upto 2500 cm²    | 3                | 5                                                    |
>      | Above 2500 cm²                 | 6                | 8                                                    |
>
>      Provided that the size of numeral and/or letters required for declaration of net weight, retail sale price, date of expiry or best before or the use by date (wherever and as applicable) and Consumer care details on the principal display panel shall be as provided in the Legal Metrology Act, 2009 (1 of 2010) and the rules made there under.

- #### The declaration must be in English or Hindi (Devanagari). Table I prescribes minimum letter heights on the Principal Display Panel (PDP). All required information, including allergens, must appear on the PDP, and allergen names must follow these display rules.

## 5. Delhi High Court Ruling (Ram Gaua Raksha Dal)

#### Q: What does the Delhi High Court ruling in Ram Gaua Raksha Dal v. Union of India (W.P.(C) 12055/2021) establish about source declaration independent of quantity?

Source: [Delhi High Court Judgment (Ram Gaua Raksha Dal v. Union of India)](https://delhihighcourt.nic.in/app/showFile/1646996691459_92139_2022.pdf/2022), W.P.(C) 12055/2021, page 2 [ paragraph 3 (point 4.) ]  
Claim :- ALLER.FSSAI.C.06  

### Direct excerpt:

>  - 4. In view of the above regulatory provisions read with FSS (Lebelling and Display) Regulations, 2020, every package of “Non Vegetarian” food having ingredients including additives, from animal sources but excluding milk or milk products, honey or bees wax or carnauba wax or shellac, shall bear a symbol and colour code to indicate that the product is Non-Vegetarian Food. It is clarified that the declaration regarding Non-Veg or Veg food is mandatory irrespective of the percentage of any ingredient in the food.

- #### Even a very small amount of an animal-derived ingredient must be declared on the label, because the rule applies regardless of quantity.

## Claims List

- #### ALLER.FSSAI.C.01 — The Labelling Display Regulations 2020 require “Contains [allergen]” declarations for specified allergens including gluten cereals, milk, eggs, fish, peanuts/tree nuts, soybeans, and sulphites (≥10 mg/kg).

- #### ALLER.FSSAI.C.02 — Allergen declaration is required when listed allergens are present, irrespective of quantity, except for sulphites (≥10 mg/kg); “may contain” labeling for cross-contamination (accidental mixing) is permitted but not mandatory.

- #### ALLER.FSSAI.C.03 —Allergen information must be declared as part of the ingredient list and presented clearly, prominently, and indelibly in English or Hindi (Devanagari).

- #### ALLER.FSSAI.C.04 —  Food labels must comply with minimum font size requirements specified by FSSAI to ensure legibility of mandatory declarations.

- #### ALLER.FSSAI.C.05 — The 2020 Labelling Regulations introduce explicit allergen declaration requirements, including “Contains” labeling and sulphite thresholds, which were not present in the 2011 framework.

- #### ALLER.FSSAI.C.06 — The Delhi High Court in Ram Gaua Raksha Dal v. Union of India held that source disclosure is required irrespective of quantity, including trace amounts.
